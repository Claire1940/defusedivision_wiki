<Section icon={Target} title="Timing, Strafes, and Rhythm">

The core skill is timing. In this game, the jump window, strafe direction, and mouse adjustment need to feel like one motion. Think of each hop as a beat in a metronome: jump, drift, land, repeat. The best players are not usually pressing more buttons; they are making fewer mistakes between inputs.

Build the habit in a safe order. First, learn to keep your camera steady. Then add strafes. Finally, chain hops only after the landing pattern feels predictable. That sequence prevents the most common problem: trying to run before you can keep the rhythm.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Set a fixed rhythm">
    Start with short hop chains and count the timing in your head. Keep the gap between jumps consistent.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Add one strafe direction">
    Practice moving left only, then right only. Separate the two until the camera movement feels deliberate.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Link the landing to the next jump">
    Watch your feet or the ground contact point. The next jump should begin as soon as the landing is stable.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Blend in angle changes">
    Once the chain is stable, change direction only at safe moments, such as after cover or during a rotation.
  </Step>
</Steps>

| Common Mistake | Why It Slows You Down | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Jumping too early | Breaks landing control | Wait for a clean contact rhythm |
| Overstrafing | Pulls you off path | Use smaller, deliberate mouse turns |
| Holding one direction | Creates predictable movement | Alternate strafes with purpose |
| Looking around too much | Hurts consistency | Keep the camera calm during chains |
| Sprinting into hops | Causes sloppy inputs | Start from a stable approach |

<Callout type="warning" title="Rhythm Warning">
If your camera swings wildly, stop chaining hops and rebuild the pattern from a single jump. Speed without control is wasted movement.
</Callout>

</Section>

<Section icon={Map} title="Map Angles and Route Choices">

Bunny hopping is most valuable when the map gives you room to breathe. Defuse Division’s bomb-site structure, mid control lanes, chokepoints, and rotation routes make some paths ideal for fast movement and others terrible for it. The trick is reading the route before you start the chain.

Use bhops to cross exposed territory, exit a fight, or rotate when the enemy team has already committed elsewhere. Do not force hops through narrow corridors where your momentum will get clipped by a wall, doorway, or hard turn. In those spots, controlled walking is usually safer.

| Situation | Best Movement Choice | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Long open rotation | Controlled bhop chain | Preserves speed across distance |
| Tight entry choke | Walk or short burst | Reduces collision and aim loss |
| Leaving a site after a pick | Short escape bhops | Helps break enemy tracking |
| Crossing mid with vision risk | Fast, clean hops | Limits time in the open |
| Approaching a corner fight | Stop hopping early | Better crosshair discipline |

<Section icon={Trophy} title="Practice Plan and Review Checklist">

A strong bunny hop habit comes from repetition, not theory. Practice the same route until you can predict the landing rhythm without thinking. Then introduce small changes: a different lane, a later jump, or a tighter strafe. That approach builds control while keeping mistakes easy to diagnose.

Keep your practice short and focused. Ten clean minutes is more useful than thirty rushed minutes. Once you can hold rhythm in a quiet setting, move the drill into live matches and use it only when the route actually deserves it.

| Day | Drill | Goal |

| Day 1 | Single-hop timing | Learn clean landings |
| Day 2 | Left strafe chains | Lock in one direction |
| Day 3 | Right strafe chains | Balance muscle memory |

| Day 5 | Live-match usage | Pick smarter bhop moments |
